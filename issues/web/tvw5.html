The last issue of the Spring '25 semester is upon us

Good lord. We're already at the last week of classes. Are you ready for summer? Because we sure are! Unfortunately though, that means this is the last issue this semester. Thank you again for supporting us throughout the last month and a week! Anyway, without further ado, a rundown of the news this week.

All seemed well and good in the first half. It had been a very nice week in the tech dumps; however, on Thursday, disaster struck in the tunnels.

We'll start with the bad news. As of Thursday, April 17th, a camera was installed in the JEC–DCC tunnel by the DCC entrance doors. It's unclear why they installed them now of all times, but we may update you on the situation further prior to the semester's end if anything changes. The hope is that this will end the same as the 9th floor of the CII stairwell, where a gate and camera were installed roughly 5–7 years ago (according to the Campus Exploration Manual), both of which have been since removed.

In better news, the JEC has had quite a large dump of electronics and cables this week, along with 3 CRTs, 1 PC monitor, and 2 TVs—one even with FM radio! Plus, the VCC has had a truckload of laptops, most just working. Read more about it in What's new in the box(es).

We at Tunnel Vision Weekly are very sad at the news of the cameras, and are hoping that this will blow over as quickly as it started. Good luck on finals, have fun over the summer, and hopefully we'll see you next fall for Tunnel Vision Weekly #6!

What's new in the box(es): end-of-semester edition

Holy moly, these last few weeks really have been the best time of the semester to go to the tech dumps. The VCC has not stopped its absolute scale of giveaways: laptop here, iPad there, let's throw in a working PC, why not? Not only that, but the JEC has had a big dump of cables, CRTs, and laptops as well. How many PCs can one building hold? We're finding out week by week as more and more stuff gets thrown out. Keep your eyes peeled, there could be a lot coming into the dumps before move-out day!

Find of the week: ooh, shiny

We had a pretty great week this week, so it was tough to pick just one thing to feature, but I think this is a great parting gift for you all as you march onto summer! Plus, I for one love how shiny these things are and think they look great in any space. You could even make it into a necklace if you really wanted to!

Where to find

You should be able to find these beauts in the DCC tech dump, just somewhere in there.

Alternatively, if you have a star-shaped bit, all you have to do is unscrew the (usually 6) screws on the top of the HDD, peel off the top—it may be screwed in, so twist it around a bunch if it's not going—then unscrew the axle, and slide it out! The VCC has a ton of HDDs left if you've got the tools!

What to do

Stare at it until you realize the meaning of life. Or hang it up somewhere as a mini-mirror, or display it as a display piece, or even use as a necklace! The possibilities really are endless with this neat part.

Tunnel Vision: Out of the Tunnel

How to have a not-so-bummer Arch summer
—by anonymous

With 2 years on campus under your belt, you no doubt know how to survive the harsh winters of upstate New York, you know how to bundle up and keep warm, but do you know how to handle RPI in the heat? During the traditional academic year (fall and spring) there is at most a month or two (in total) of truly warm weather, and the rest of the time is either cold or unbearably cold. While there is a lot to be said for sledding and snowball fights, there is only so much time you can partake in winter activities before you lose an appendage to frostbite. During the arch summer, clear skies and 95 degrees Fahrenheit (that's 35 Celsius) is the new norm. Spending time outdoors in the sun is a privilege not easily afforded to RPI students, so let this be your guide to making the most of your summertime while at RPI.

Beating the Heat

Nothing beats the heat like an ice cream cone (or cup). If you venture into downtown Troy, the Dutch Udder serves up a wide variety of rotating flavors, from mint chip to sweet potato pie, as well as specialty items like root beer floats and donut sandwiches. If the thought of having to walk up the approach in the heat to get back to campus is deterring you, the closest Stewarts is only about 5 minutes' walking distance and always has solid ice cream options, offered on the cone or in pints. If you're looking to venture out a bit further, The Snowman, established in the 1950s, offers a variety of homemade ice creams, yogurts and sherberts. A long-standing institution in Troy, and only open seasonally, it's definitely worth checking out while you can.

No Car, No Problem

Every Saturday morning the Troy Farmers' Market takes over downtown. This impressively-sized event is a great way to spend a morning before that afternoon heat starts to beat down. Once it does start to beat down, head for a stroll along the Troy waterfront, taking advantage of the cool breeze coming off the Hudson. If the river's breeze isn't cutting it, step into The Arts Center of the Capital Region located conveniently at 265 River St for some air conditioning and funky art exhibits. If you prefer dirt over pavement, less than 5 minutes from campus at the bottom of 13th sits Prospect Park. Complete with scenic views of downtown, basketball courts, tennis courts and a massive disk golf course, this is my personal favorite place to spend a sunny day in Troy.

If you're looking for something more refreshing, there is a spot not too far from Prospect commonly referred to by students as "The Gorge." 45 Linden Ave is what you'll put into Google Maps to find this place. It is down the trail at the bottom of the parking lot. It's a quaint swimming hole enjoyed by RPI students throughout the years.

Danger: Going to the Gorge is trespassing, which is illegal. We are not responsible for any criminal charges that may occur if you go there.

Enjoying New York State

New York has a beautiful collection of state parks. Peebles Island, Thacher, Grafton, and Cherry Plain are all relatively nearby. A longer ways away but well worth the trip is Taghkanic State Park. In Durham, under Stone Bridge Road (put Stone Bridge Road into Google Maps), there is a swimming hole and a waterfall. It's deep enough at certain spots to jump into, with multiple outcroppings from which to jump. If you're looking for a more mild nature experience, Buckingham Lake Park, Lincoln Park, and Washington Park are all located across the river in Albany, the capital of this great state. There is also a huge collection of museums in Albany. The New York State Museum, The Capitol Building, University Art Museum, Albany Center Gallery, and Throop Pharmacy Museum are all free to the public and make for a great activity when it feels like there's just no escape from the heat.

Good Luck, Have Fun!

Less than 10 years old, the Arch program has become an infamous part of the RPI experience. There's no sugarcoating it: you're facing a standard 15 week semester's worth of coursework condensed into about 12 weeks. It's probably not the summer break you were hoping for. At the end of the day you'll get out of Arch what you put in. Hopefully now that you know what Troy and the surrounding areas have to offer, you can get the most out of this experience.
